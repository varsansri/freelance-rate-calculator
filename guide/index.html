How to Calculate Your Freelance Rate — 2024 Pricing Guide

Most freelancers undercharge — significantly. They set a rate based on what feels comfortable to say out loud, not what they actually need to survive, grow, and build a sustainable business. This guide teaches you to calculate your rate mathematically, so you charge what you're actually worth.

The Three Freelance Rate Tiers

Minimum
Covers bills, nothing else. Survival rate.
Recommended
Covers bills + savings + business costs.
Premium
Market rate for experienced professionals.

The Freelance Rate Formula

Step 1: Annual income target (net after tax) Step 2: Add 30% for taxes Step 3: Add annual business expenses Step 4: Add annual savings/retirement goal Step 5: Divide by billable hours per year Billable hours = 52 weeks × 40 hrs × 0.6 (only ~60% of "working" hours are billable) = ~1,248 billable hours/year Hourly Rate = (Annual Need) ÷ 1,248

Why Only 60% Billable Hours?

This is the number most new freelancers get wrong. Your 40-hour work week doesn't convert to 40 billable hours. A realistic breakdown:

Sales, proposals, calls4–6 hrs❌ No
Marketing, content, social3–5 hrs❌ No

Warning: If your calculated rate feels "too high," it's because you're comparing yourself to employees. An employee earning $50k/year has taxes, benefits, office space, and equipment paid by the employer. A freelancer billing the equivalent must cover all of those themselves. Your rate should be 1.5–2× the equivalent employee salary.

Should You Charge Hourly or Per Project?

Project pricing is almost always better for freelancers above beginner level. When you price per project, your income scales with your skill and efficiency — not your hours. The faster you work, the more you earn per hour. Many experienced freelancers charge 3–5× their hourly rate when packaging work as projects.

Golden rule: Calculate your minimum rate using the formula, then double it. That's your starting ask. You can always negotiate down, but you can never negotiate up from a too-low first quote.
